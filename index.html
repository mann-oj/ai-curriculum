How AI Learns
AI doesn't get programmed with rules — it learns from examples. This week you'll understand exactly how that works, and you'll train your very own AI model using Google Teachable Machine.

💡 Key Concepts
  • Training data — AI learns by seeing thousands of labelled examples

⚠️ Why This Week Matters
AI bias is real. In 2018, a facial recognition system used by US law enforcement was found to misidentify Black women at error rates 34 times higher than for white men. The cause? Biased training data.
Deepfakes are growing. In 2023, synthetic media incidents increased by 900%. Learning to spot them isn't optional anymore.

✗ Weak Prompt
"Explain photosynthesis."

🛡 SAFETY WITH AI

A dedicated section — read this before using any AI tool

Why safety matters at your age specifically

AI tools are designed for adults, but millions of teenagers use them every day. That's not a reason to avoid them — but it is a reason to know exactly how to protect yourself. The four areas below are things every 13-14 year old should understand before typing a single prompt.

🔒 Don't share personal info

AI chat tools store and may use your conversations for training. Treat them like a public notice board — never type anything you wouldn't want a stranger to see.

Never enter your full name, school, address, or phone number
Never share passwords or answers to security questions
Avoid uploading photos that include your face, location, or school uniform
Don't share personal problems or family details — a trusted adult is better for that
🕵️ Spot AI misinformation & deepfakes

AI can generate convincing fake images, videos, and text. Knowing how to spot them is one of the most important digital skills you can have right now.

Look for unnatural hands, blurry edges around faces, or odd lighting in images
AI text often sounds confident but can be completely wrong — always verify with a second source
Use tools like Google Reverse Image Search or TinEye to check if an image is real
If a video or audio clip seems shocking or "too perfect" — be suspicious before sharing
🧠 Mental health & over-reliance

AI chatbots can feel like friends — they're always available, never judge you, and seem to understand. But they are not people, and relying on them emotionally can get in the way of real connections.

Using AI for homework help is fine — but always try the problem yourself first
If you find yourself going to AI for emotional support regularly, talk to a real person you trust instead
AI praise is not real — it's designed to be encouraging, not honest
Notice if AI use is cutting into sleep, social time, or things you used to enjoy — that's worth paying attention to
🌐 Online safety & data privacy

Every time you use an AI tool, data is collected. Understanding what happens to it — and how to protect yourself — puts you in control.

Most free AI tools require you to be 13+ — but many also require a parent's permission under 18
Read (or ask an adult to explain) the privacy policy before creating an account
Check if the tool lets you delete your conversation history — and use that option
Avoid using AI tools on public or shared devices where your session might be visible to others

🛡 SAFETY ACTIVITY — REFLECT
Your Personal AI Safety Checklist
Before you use any new AI tool for the first time, run through this checklist. You can copy this into your notes and keep it handy.
1 Do I know who made this tool and do I trust them?
2 Am I about to type anything personal — name, school, location, feelings? If yes, stop.
3 Does this tool have an age requirement and do I meet it?
4 If the AI tells me something important — have I checked it with another source?
5 Am I using this to help me learn — or to avoid learning altogether?

YOUR TAKEAWAYS
6 Weeks. Here's What You Now Know.

A one-page summary of everything you covered — save it, screenshot it, print it out. This is your cheat sheet for talking about AI confidently.

WEEK 1
What Even Is AI?
AI is software that learns from examples instead of following fixed rules. It's already in your phone, your music app, your search engine, and your social feed — usually working quietly in the background without you noticing.
WEEK 2
How AI Learns
AI learns by being shown thousands of labelled examples — a process called training. The more examples it sees, the better it gets at spotting patterns. You trained your own image classifier using Google Teachable Machine and saw this happen live.
WEEK 3
Inside the Neural Network
Neural networks are layers of connected nodes that process information, inspired loosely by the human brain. Data flows in, gets transformed layer by layer, and an answer comes out the other side. AI tools like ChatGPT are built on enormous versions of this same idea.
WEEK 4
AI Ethics & Society
When AI is trained on biased data, it produces biased results — and real people get hurt by that. Deepfakes, surveillance, and automation all raise hard questions about fairness and responsibility. Knowing about these issues makes you a more informed citizen, not just a better tech user.
WEEK 5
Using AI Tools & Staying Safe
The quality of what you get from AI depends heavily on how you ask — a specific, detailed prompt almost always beats a vague one. AI also makes things up confidently, so always verify. And never share personal information with any AI tool — treat every conversation like a public space.
WEEK 6
The Future of AI
AI is moving fast — into medicine, science, creative work, and every industry you can think of. Careers in AI aren't just for coders — there are roles in ethics, policy, communication, design, and research. The people who understand AI will help shape where it goes next. That can be you.
📖 Key Terms to Remember
Artificial Intelligence (AI) — software that learns from data to perform tasks
Training data — the labelled examples used to teach an AI model
Machine learning — the method of teaching AI through examples rather than rules
Neural network — layers of connected nodes that process and transform data
Large Language Model (LLM) — AI trained on huge amounts of text to predict and generate language
AI bias — when unfair patterns in training data cause an AI to produce unfair results
Deepfake — AI-generated synthetic media that makes someone appear to say or do something they didn't
Prompt — the instruction or question you give an AI tool
Hallucination — when AI confidently states something that is factually wrong
Generative AI — AI that can create new text, images, audio, or video
